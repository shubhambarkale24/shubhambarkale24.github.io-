Strategy

If I had to start the preparation again

A long preparation teaches you more in hindsight than it ever does in the moment. Here is what I'd change if I could begin once more — and the one thing I wouldn't touch.

This is placeholder copy in your own voice's shape — replace it with the real essay whenever you're ready. The structure below is a good default for a guidance post: a short honest opening, a few clearly-headed sections, and a closing that points the reader to one concrete next step.

1. Build the syllabus into muscle memory first

Before touching a single standard book, I'd spend a week doing nothing but reading the syllabus until I could recite its shape. Everything you study later is easier to file when you already know which drawer it belongs in.

2. Treat answer-writing as a daily habit, not a final-phase skill

The single biggest gap between knowing and scoring is the ability to write a clear, structured answer under time pressure. Start early, even when it feels premature.

You don't rise to the level of your knowledge; you fall to the level of your practice.

3. Choose fewer sources, revise them more

The temptation is to collect. The discipline is to revise. A handful of well-revised sources beats a library you've read once.

4. Protect your health like it's part of the syllabus

Sleep, movement, and a little stillness are not indulgences in a multi-year preparation — they are what keeps the engine running long enough to finish.

5. Find one honest reader

Whether a mentor, a peer, or a study group — someone who will tell you the truth about your answers is worth more than any test series.

The one thing I'd keep

The habit of writing a short reflection at the end of each week: what I studied, what I understood, and what I was avoiding. It kept me honest with myself, and that honesty is, in the end, what the examination is really testing.
